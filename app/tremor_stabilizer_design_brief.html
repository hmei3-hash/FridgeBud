Hand Tremor Stabilizer — design brief

Project info
Design name
Hand Tremor Stabilizer
Competition
ASME × WOOF3D CADathon 2026
Need
Who is the user and what problem do they face?
Elderly individuals and people with motor control disorders (e.g. Parkinson's disease, essential tremor) experience involuntary hand shaking that prevents them from performing everyday precision tasks — writing, drawing, eating, and basic grip-dependent activities. As tremor severity increases, many lose independence in tasks they could previously perform without assistance.
Core need statement
People with hand tremors need a lightweight, body-attached mechanical stabilizer that dampens involuntary shaking while preserving intentional movement, so they can regain independence in precision tasks like writing, drawing, and daily living activities.
Constraints
Materials must be soft / skin-safe where in contact with the body (TPU padding, fabric straps)
Must attach securely to the wrist/hand without restricting blood flow
Low total weight — target under 300 g to avoid fatigue
No sharp external edges; all load-bearing parts enclosed or guarded
Should be adjustable to fit a range of wrist sizes
Soft-contact materials Body-attached Low weight Safety-first Adjustable fit
Design Purpose
Design goal — what it must achieve
Design a wrist-mounted mechanical stabilizer that uses counterweights or a gyroscopic gimbal mechanism to passively dampen high-frequency involuntary tremors, while allowing full intentional range of motion. The device must include an adjustable sensitivity mechanism so the user can tune the damping threshold to their tremor severity.
Success criteria — how completion is measured
1. The stabilization mechanism visibly reduces simulated tremor motion in CAD animation
2. No uncontrolled moving parts that could cause pinching, catching, or injury during normal use
3. A new user can understand how to don and use the device within a short learning period (intuitive attachment mechanism)
4. The model demonstrates full range of intentional wrist motion is preserved
Core design intent — why this approach
Millions of elderly people progressively lose the ability to write, draw, or perform grip-dependent tasks as tremor worsens. A purely mechanical, passive gyroscopic solution requires no batteries, no software, and no calibration — making it highly accessible and low-maintenance for the target population. Secondary applications include surgical instrument stabilization and fine-craft use. The passive mechanism also means the device cannot malfunction electronically, improving long-term safety and reliability.
